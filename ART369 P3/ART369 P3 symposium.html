Workshop
Tell Your Tale: Filmmaking 101, directed Masaaki Miyazawa

9:30am - 11:30am

Ian Cheney, an Emmy-nominated and Peabody Award-winning documentary filmmaker, will introduce festival attendees to the craft of environmental film making. It doesn’t matter if you’ve never done it before! His approachable and engaging workshop on how to make your own film will draw from his experience composing his early films from scratch with little technical training. The main goal of the workshop is to inspire anyone who has considered picking up a camera to tell a story, environmental or non-environmental. It will help give you the initial conceptual and technical tools to get started.

Workshop
Stories for our Time: Environmental Storytelling through Film

12:00pm - 2:00pm

In this two-part workshop, Paul Lussier will lead a workshop on conveying complex and compelling environmental messages through film. Paul is an Emmy-nominated executive producer who is spearheading new environmental communication frameworks worldwide. He will discuss the history and transformation of environmental film from the early 90s and illuminate techniques to artful and persuasive storytelling through film. The second part of the workshop features David Gelber, Executive Producer of the Showtime television series Years of Living Dangerously, (2014 Emmy for best non-fiction series winner), which focuses on the global and domestic impacts of climate change. Gelber will provide a critical discussion of the production decisions involved in the series and how these impacted the final product. Overall, the workshop will highlight the unique role that film has in bringing awareness to environmental problems worldwide.


Workshop Leaders

Ian Cheney is an Emmy-nominated and Peabody Award-winning documentary filmmaker. He received Bachelor’s and Master’s degrees from Yale University and its School of Forestry & Environmental Studies. His films and collaborations include King Corn (2007), The Greening of Southie (2008) Big River (2009), Truck Farm (2010), The City Dark (2011), The Melungeons (2012), and The Search for General Tso (2014). In 2011 he and longtime collaborator Curt Ellis received the Heinz Award in recognition of their work using humor and innovative programming to engage people about sustainable food. A co-founder of FoodCorps and a director/producer at Wicked Delicate Films LLC, he is currently a 2014-2015 Knight fellow at MIT.


Paul Lussier is a researcher, educator and lecturer on science communications and public policy at Yale University, where he is spearheading innovative knowledge co-production communication and strategy models for public policy interest, uptake and implementation. He is also the founder and CEO of Me2U Media, a New York-based collaborative of scientists, journalists, policy experts and communication personnel dedicated to the research and development of next generation science communication media. He lectures on the interface of science, culture and public policy at institutions worldwide. Lussier’s clients and collaborations count among leading science and research academies, government institutions and media conglomerates worldwide. He is also the founder of Citizen Global, the world’s leading collaborative online media platform for video-enabled, data-sharing global working groups for natural and social sciences,and for policy research, development and implementation. Lussier is also an Emmy Award-winning executive producer and screenwriter responsible for writing, directing and producing over two hundred hours of science, environmental and political science programming for The Discovery Channel, Warner Bros. Entertainment, HBO, and all major broadcast networks, including development of the ten-part climate series Eleventh Hour.


David Gelber served as Ed Bradley’s producer at 60 Minutes® for twenty-five years, during which he won every major journalism award, including a Peabody, two DuPont Awards and eight Emmy® Awards. During the nineties, Gelber was executive producer of Peter Jennings Reporting at ABC News®. He spent two winters in Sarajevo producing documentaries on the Bosnian Conflict. He returned to CBS News® in 1996 to head the Ed Bradley Unit. His story on medical charlatans who peddle bogus stem cell therapy to patients dying of ALS won the Emmy for Best Investigative Story of 2010. Before leaving 60 Minutes, Gelber did two stories on climate change: one on the dramatic increase in wildfires sweeping the American west and the other on the so-called ‘clean coal’ campaign. Gelber and his colleague Joel Bach left 60 Minutes four years ago to focus entirely on climate change. Years of Living Dangerously, which won the Emmy for Best Nonfiction Series of 2014, was the result. Gelber and Bach are now beginning production on the second season of Years, which will air on the National Geographic Channel next year. Gelber has served on the Swarthmore College Board of Managers for 12 years. He and his wife Kyoko have two children: Maya, 10, and Clara, 6. They live in Garrison, NY.
